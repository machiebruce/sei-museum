The Black-winged Stilt is an elegant and unmistakable wader, famous for its disproportionately long legs relative to its body. It is one of the species that has benefitted the most from wetland protection in Italy.

#### **Distribution and Habitat**

**Global Distribution**  
Cosmopolitan species distributed in temperate and tropical zones of Europe, Africa, Asia and the Americas. In Europe it is a summer migrant, wintering in Africa (trans-Saharan), although wintering cases in the Mediterranean are increasing.

**Preferred Habitat**  
Prefers very shallow freshwater or brackish waters with muddy bottoms: salt pans, rice paddies, marshes and coastal lagoons.

**Presence in Sicily**  
Regular breeder and common migrant. Some individuals winter on the island if climatic conditions are mild.

**Status in the Salt Pans**  
The Trapani and Ettore Infersa Salt Pans are an excellent nesting site for this species. Starting in spring, the Black-winged Stilts enliven the pools with their incessant calls to defend their territory.

**Morphological Characteristics**  
Slim and graceful wader.
Length 33-38 cm, wingspan 67-83 cm, weight 160-200 g.
Characterized by very long pink legs (up to 30 cm) that allow it to walk in deeper water than other waders. The beak is black, straight and thin. Pure white body plumage with black wings.

**Sexual Dimorphism**  
Slight but visible. The adult male has glossy black wings with greenish reflections and often a black- or grey-spotted head in summer. The female has dark brown-blackish wings (less glossy) and generally a whiter head.

**Behavior**  
Very loud and aggressive in nest defense. It emits a piercing alarm call ("kik-kik-kik") and performs dive-bombing flights against predators (or human intruders) to drive them away (mobbing behavior).

**Feeding**  
Feeds on aquatic insects, larvae, small crustaceans, molluscs and tadpoles, which it captures by walking nimbly in the water and pecking at the surface or in the mud.

**Reproduction**  
Nests in loose colonies, often near Avocets or Terns. The nest may be a simple depression in the mud or a more elaborate structure of plant stems if there is risk of water level fluctuation.
Lays 3-4 spotted eggs, perfectly camouflaged against the ground. The chicks are precocial (they walk soon after hatching).

<div class="species-description-grid">
  <div>
    <strong>Historical Threats</strong>
    <p>Wetland reclamation and agricultural conversion drastically reduced its habitat in the last century.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <strong>Current Threats</strong>
    <p>Sudden water level changes (including from anthropogenic management of salt pans/dams) can flood nests. Predation by gulls, corvids and rats is a risk factor for eggs and chicks. Tourist disturbance in nesting areas.</p>
  </div>
</div>

**Conservation Status**  
IUCN Global: LC (Least Concern)
The Italian population is clearly increasing thanks to legal protection and oasis management.
