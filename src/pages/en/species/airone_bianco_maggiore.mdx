The Great White Egret is an elegant wading bird of the Ardeidae family. With its entirely white plumage and majestic bearing, it is one of the most iconic species of Mediterranean marshlands. During the breeding season it develops long ornamental feathers on its back and its beak takes on a more vivid coloration.

#### **Distribution and Habitat**

**Global Distribution**

The Great White Egret has a cosmopolitan distribution, present on every continent except Antarctica. In Europe, the species is found from the Mediterranean area to the northern temperate regions, with significant populations in Hungary, Romania, southern Russia and the Netherlands.

**Preferred Habitat**

It prefers freshwater or brackish wetlands with emergent vegetation: coastal lagoons, marshes, rice paddies, ponds, river deltas and salt pans. It needs shallow water (20-50 cm) rich in fish and with trees or reedbeds for nesting.

**Presence in Sicily**

In Sicily it is present year-round with resident and migratory populations. The Trapani Salt Pans are among the most important sites for the species, together with the Vendicari Reserve and coastal wetlands. During winter it concentrates in coastal wetlands where it finds favorable climatic conditions and abundant prey.

**Status in the Salt Pans**

At the Ettore Infersa Salt Pans it can be observed throughout the year, with presence peaks during migratory periods (March-April and September-November). It uses the pools as feeding areas and the surrounding reedbeds for resting. It does not nest at the site but regularly frequents the area.

**Morphological Characteristics**

Large heron with entirely white plumage in all seasons. Length 85-102 cm, wingspan 140-170 cm, weight 900-1500 g. Beak bright yellow in winter, becoming orange-reddish during the breeding season. Long dark legs, S-shaped neck when at rest.

**Sexual Dimorphism**

Absent. Males and females are morphologically identical, with males only slightly larger. During the breeding season both sexes develop long ornamental feathers (aigrettes) on the back and the plumage takes on a softer texture.

**Behavior**

Solitary and patient hunter, remains motionless in shallow water waiting for prey. Majestic flight with slow, regular wingbeats, neck folded in an S-shape. Harsh, rasping calls, emitted mainly during flight or in situations of disturbance.

**Feeding**

Diet based mainly on fish (mullet, eels, sea bass), but also includes amphibians, aquatic reptiles, crustaceans, aquatic insects and occasionally small mammals. Hunting technique: motionless waiting followed by a rapid beak strike.

**Reproduction**

Nests in mixed colonies with other herons, building large nests on trees or reedbeds. Lays 3-5 bluish-green eggs between March and May. Incubation 25-26 days, fledging at 42-49 days. Both parents share incubation and parental care.

    <strong>Historical Threats</strong>
    <p>Intensive hunting for the ornamental feather trade (aigrettes) between 1870 and 1920, which brought the species to the brink of extinction in Europe. Large-scale wetland reclamation after World War II. Use of organochlorine pesticides (DDT) in the 1950s-1970s causing die-offs and reduced reproductive success.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <strong>Current Threats</strong>
    <p>Anthropic disturbance in nesting and feeding areas. Water pollution and bioaccumulation of heavy metals and microplastics. Climate change altering migratory phenology and prey availability. Collision with power lines during migration. Nest predation by corvids.</p>
  </div>
</div>

**Conservation Status**

IUCN Global: LC (Least Concern)  
Italian Red List: LC (Least Concern)  
EU Birds Directive: Annex I (species of special conservation interest)  
Bern Convention: Annex II (strictly protected species)

The European population is increasing after the drastic decline of the 19th century, with approximately 37,000-54,000 nesting pairs. In Italy the nesting population is estimated at 800-1,200 pairs, increasing in recent decades.
