The Greater Flamingo is undoubtedly the symbol species of the salt pans. With its long legs, sinuous neck, and coloration ranging from white to deep pink, it offers breathtaking natural spectacles, especially when gathered in large flocks.

#### **Distribution and Habitat**

**Global Distribution**
Species widespread in Africa, the Indian subcontinent, the Middle East, and southern Europe (Mediterranean area). In Europe, the most important colonies are found in the Camargue (France), Spain, Sardinia, and Apulia.

**Preferred Habitat**
Closely tied to brackish coastal lagoons, salt pans, and shallow salt lakes. Prefers large open water bodies where it can filter the seabed undisturbed.

**Presence in Sicily**
Sicily is a crucial stopover for flamingo migration routes. Populations have increased significantly in recent decades, with nesting attempts in some protected areas (e.g., Priolo).

**Status in the Salt Pans**
At Saline Ettore Infersa and the Stagnone Nature Reserve, it is an iconic species present year-round, with very high numbers during migrations and winter. The salt pan basins provide the ideal feeding habitat thanks to the high concentration of small crustaceans.

**Morphological Characteristics**
Large unmistakable bird.
Length 120-145 cm, wingspan 140-165 cm, weight 2-4 kg.
Adults have pinkish-white plumage with bright red wing coverts and black flight feathers (visible in flight). The beak is pink with a black tip, curved downward for filtering water. Juveniles are grayish-brown and gradually acquire pink coloration through carotenoid pigments in their food.

**Sexual Dimorphism**
Slight. Males are generally larger and taller than females, but distinction in the wild is difficult.

**Behavior**
Gregarious and social species, lives in groups that can number thousands of individuals. Its call resembles that of geese. During courtship, it performs spectacular synchronized group "dances."

**Feeding**
Highly specialized. Feeds by filtering water and mud with its inverted beak. Diet consists of small crustaceans (such as *Artemia salina*, responsible for the pink color), mollusks, annelids, insect larvae, and microscopic algae.

**Reproduction**
Nests in dense colonies on muddy islets in shallow waters. The nest is a cone of mud up to 30-40 cm high.
Lays a single white egg (rarely two). Incubation lasts about 28-31 days. Chicks soon gather in "nurseries" watched over by some adults.

    <strong>Historical Threats</strong>
    <p>In the past, egg collection and colony disturbance limited expansion. Wetland reclamation reduced available habitat.</p>
  </div>
  
  <div>
    <strong>Current Threats</strong>
    <p>Extremely sensitive to human disturbance (disrespectful photographers, drones, low-flying aircraft) which can cause nest abandonment. Erosion of nesting islets and water pollution are additional risks. Power lines along migration routes cause collisions.</p>

**Conservation Status**
IUCN Global: LC (Least Concern)
Expanding in the Mediterranean.
